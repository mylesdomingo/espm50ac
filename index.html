Background

PG&E, the Electric Company that serves California, had desperately tried to stop new fires but shutting off power to prevent lines from catching fire. In a technological world, lack of access to power resulted to mass disruption to the lives of citizens. Nearly a million people were affected. Work and classes are cancelled, facilities are temporarily closed — entire cities come to a complete halt.

Yet, despite several rounds of deliberate blackouts to reduce wildfire risk, it failed. The Getty Fire, presumed to be started by a downed power line, ignited near Los Angeles in November 2019, forcing the evacuation of thousands of people. It seems that wildfire season is getting more and more out of control every year. Is it?

Natural Fire is Not The Problem, Humans are.

Fires are a normal player in a forest’s ecosystem — a cycle that allow trees to burn, rejuvenate, make way for life anew. However, the suppression of fires in the past have done more harm than good — leaving behind stacks of fuel to accumulate and increase hazardous conditions. Over the scale of decades, these fires can act on a scale that is beyond human control, as seen in the next section.

150,000 acres.

18,000 buildings.

85 lives taken.

Paradise Lost.

These are the results of Camp Fire in 2018 , the states recently most deadliest and destructive wildfire. The fire burned throughout a small town named Paradise near Chico, California. The effects of its rampage has left properties destroyed, thousands homeless, and loved ones lost.

The reason for Camp Fires destruction? Nearly perfect conditions for fire to spread. Climate change over the past decade has increased temperatures, dried crops and vegetation, increased winds. The influx of organic fuel and oxygen was made this wildfire so potent.

How it Affects Us - Air Pollution

According to Vox, most wildfire deaths are not from the fires themselves but the inhalation of smoke that it produces. The particle matter and gases emitted by combustion pose a common health threat. These particles, denoted as PM2.5 for being smaller than 2.5 microns in diameters, spread far distances through airborne travel. When inhaled, it increases an individuals risk to asthma attacks and lung cancer.

Especially for the youth, elderly, and those with pre-existing conditions, PM2.5 is a serious concern. Areas like Oakland and Fresno have reach up to 150-250 AQI, far above safe air conditions. Some of the worst areas affected by air pollution, like Sacramento, have declared public health emergencies. N95 respirator masks are a common preventative measure in areas of high pollution. But these pose a serious socioeconomic concern — how do we ensure adequate access and distribution to all those affected. Limited quantity, and additional financial costs pose problems for those especially in poorer socioeconomic classes.

As fires grow, so will air quality worsen. Massive wildfires have led to a mass decrease in air quality throughout the united states. And as people continue to build closer to areas that burn, the more property that is at risk of destruction, and the more lives affected by fire and pollution. So, what can we, as individuals do?
